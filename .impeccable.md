## Design Context

### Users
**Primary**: Cornell University instructors and TAs. They use CMSX while managing courses — grading submissions, creating assignments, posting announcements, and monitoring student progress. Context of use: focused, task-driven, often time-pressured (grading deadlines, office hours). Secondary: students consuming grades and announcements.

**Job to be done**: Get in, complete the administrative task (grade a batch, publish an assignment, check a grade dispute), and get out. Efficiency and clarity over exploration.

### Brand Personality
**Three words**: Efficient, Credible, Composed

Voice: Professional but not cold. Institutional enough to feel trustworthy, modern enough to not feel like legacy software. No humor, no gamification, no filler copy — every word earns its place.

Emotional goal: Confidence. Staff should feel in control, never confused or overwhelmed. The system should work *with* them, not demand their attention.

### Aesthetic Direction
**Visual tone**: Modern institutional — clean, high-contrast, information-dense without feeling cluttered. Think a well-designed internal tool for a serious organization: Bloomberg Terminal meets Linear, not Notion meets Duolingo.

**Theme**: Light mode primary. White surfaces, tinted neutrals, strong typographic hierarchy.

**References**: Linear (precision, keyboard-first efficiency), GOV.UK Design System (institutional credibility, accessibility), Vercel dashboard (clean data density).

**Anti-references**:
- Consumer apps (TikTok, Instagram) — no attention-seeking patterns, no dopamine loops
- Canvas/Blackboard — no cluttered legacy LMS density
- Generic SaaS dashboards — avoid interchangeable startup aesthetics

**Colors**: Cornell red (`#B31B1B`) as a restrained accent (not dominant). Teal/blue primary (`#006699`) is established — keep it but deepen intentionality. Neutrals should be cool-tinted, not warm.

### Design Principles
1. **Clarity over cleverness** — Every UI choice should reduce cognitive load, not showcase design skill. If it needs explaining, redesign it.
2. **Density with breathing room** — Instructors work with data tables and lists. Dense is fine; cramped is not. Whitespace should be *earned* by grouping, not sprinkled decoratively.
3. **Progressive disclosure** — Show the essentials; hide the advanced. Power users find depth through interaction, not confrontation.
4. **Accessibility is non-negotiable** — WCAG AA minimum. 4.5:1 contrast ratios, full keyboard navigation, no color-only information encoding.
5. **Institutional trust signals** — Cornell identity should be present but restrained. This is a tool, not a brand campaign.
